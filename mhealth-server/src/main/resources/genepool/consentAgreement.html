Tissue Sampling for Research
Research using tissues (including blood samples, and specifically DNA) is an important way to try to understand human disease. You have been given this information because the investigators want to include your tissues in a research project and because they want to save the samples for future research. There are several things you should know before allowing your tissues to be studied.

Your sample will be stored in a -80 freezer with a unique study ID instead of your name, and may be used either in a specific genetic research study now, saved for future undetermined research on a range of diseases, or both. Participation in this study or future research studies is entirely optional, and at any time you may withdraw your sample so that it is not used for any future research

Any tissues you have donated which are used in research may result in new products, tests or discoveries. In some instances, these may have potential commercial value and may be developed and owned by the Investigators, Stanford University and/or others. Donors of tissues do not retain any property rights to the materials. Therefore, you would not share in any financial benefits from these products, tests or discoveries.

Tissue Sampling for Genetic Testing

Once you provide consent to participate in GenePool, a lab order is automatically placed in your medical record. You simply walk into any SHC blood draw area and a member of the SHC blood draw team will collect 3 tablespoons of blood from you. The blood draw order is active for 6 months, so you have the convenience of completing this part at a time that is more convenient for you. You may also submit a urine sample.

As part of the analysis on your samples, the investigators will do a research based form of genetic testing. Genetic research studies genes and gene characteristics, many of which are transmitted by parents to children. This research may include looking at not only the genetic information in your tissue sample but also relating it to other information, such as your individual and family medical histories, prior test results in your medical record, reactions to medications and treatment, and other personal characteristics. Which of these are studied and the manner in which they are examined will vary depending on what research studies your sample is used in. After genetic testing is performed, is also possible that your genetic data will be put into a database held securely at Stanford, and which could be used to perform additional studies of the DNA data without requiring an additional blood sample from you. If this is done, your genetic data will be linked only to the unique study ID (and not your name or hospital medical record), and the data will be restricted to approved researchers and research projects within the university. Your data will be encrypted and sent to a secure database, with your name replaced by a random code.

Sharing of medical and genetic information and tissue samples

It is possible that we will enter into collaborative studies with other academic institutions or private entities that requires the sharing of your medical record information, genetic information, DNA samples, or other tissue samples. The medical record information will be stripped of all identifying information including your name and hospital number, and a unique study number provided instead. The code linking study number to your name and medical record number will remain confidential at Stanford. If federal grant funding will be employed for studies using your tissues, it may require that your genetic information (and that of every other study subject) will be deposited in a large database (for example, the database of genotypes and phenotypes, dbGAP) at the National Institutes of Health (NIH). Once your data is deposited into this database, we cannot withdraw your data, even if you withdraw from this study. However, access to these databases is highly restricted, with only qualified researchers gaining access to the data. If deposited into an NIH database, your data will become part of US government records and could be subject to requests under the Freedom of Information Act. NIH plans to contest any such requests for identifiable information, but their decision could be appealed. Although the NIH will not have any direct identifiers, it is conceivable that law enforcement agencies could request access to the de-identified data and search for matches to DNA specimens collected by the police. Again, there would be no information included in the NIH database that could be easily linked to your name or contact information, but all genome information should be considered potentially identifiable.

Genetic testing results from the research studies

The results of the study of your samples will be used for research purposes only, and you will not generally be told the results of the genetic research that is performed using your sample. In some rare cases, the researchers will identify genetic information that may clearly impact your health or medical care, and where clinically-validated testing is available to confirm this finding, and the associated condition can be treated or medically managed. If this occurs, and if you indicate (below) that you wish to know this information, the protocol director will notify your treating physician. While this research data should not be used on its own to direct clinical care, you and your physician may then decide whether you wish to pursue clinically-validated testing. Regarding informing you of the any genetic research results, you should understand the following:

  • Most genetic information that is obtained as part of genetic research is too limited to understand its consequences, and this is why results are not disclosed;
  • There is a small chance that you may be determined to carry a gene that predicts high risk for a particular disease; some of these diseases can be treated, and for some there is no current treatment;
  • If you learn that you carry a gene for a disease, other relatives might also carry the gene, and you should discuss with your treating physician the best way(s) to consider informing them.
  • Possible risks of knowing genetic testing results include: anxiety; other psychological distress; and the possibility of insurance and job discrimination. A possible risk of not knowing actionable genetic test results includes being unaware of the need for surveillance or treatment. These risks differ for each specific disease.
  • A Federal law, the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act of 2008 (GINA), generally makes it illegal for health insurance companies, group health plans, and employers with 15 or more employees to discriminate against you based on your knowledge of predictive genetic test results or family history. This law does not currently protect against discrimination for life insurance, long term care insurance or disability insurance.

Urine Sample

You also have the option to provide a urine sample in addition to a blood sample. A large range of metabolites are excreted through urine, thus urine samples provide an easy, non-invasive way to screen for potential disease biomarkers. A disease biomarker is a protein or small molecule that is useful for diagnosing, classifying or monitoring disease. The biomarkers found in urine complement those found in blood allowing researchers to have a more accurate representation of the disease state.
